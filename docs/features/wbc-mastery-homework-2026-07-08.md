### **Ruleset and grinder**

The biggest equipment change is real and important. WCC announced the **2026–2029 WBrC Qualified Filter Grinder Sponsor** as the **Option-O LAGOM 01**. The 2026 season was the first year standardized sponsor-provided grinders became mandatory in both Compulsory and Open Service rounds.

WCC’s grinder procedure says all provided grinders are prepared to the same specs, use seasoned 102 mm burrs, and are cleaned / reset between competitors. Competitors bringing their own grinders at WBrC must use the same approved model and configuration and pass onsite inspection.

For 2026 USBrC, the domestic rule was stricter onstage: competitors were allowed to use their own grinder **backstage only**, but inside the competition area and during competition time they had to use the provided sponsored grinder.

Practical answer: start LAGOM 01-specific prep now, but verify the exact 2027 USBrC grinder in the domestic rules / host packet. The WBrC sponsor cycle points to LAGOM 01 through 2029, but USCC still says the sponsored grinder will be announced on the competition website or by email.

### **Competition logistics: provided vs. brought**

**Provided in 2026 USBrC**

The competition area included an equipment table, service / judging table, water heating devices, official grinder, standardized service vessels, cleaning brushes, trash / compost, liquid bucket, and one waiter’s cart.

Hot water was provided by hot water machine or kettles. The hot water machine was set between **96.0°C and 98.5°C**. Competitors were not allowed to adjust the hot water machine.

For Compulsory Service, competitors had to use the provided water. For Open Service, competitors were permitted to use their own brew water, but needed to notify the event / stage manager, provide samples for head judge evaluation, and use their own heating device if not using provided water.

**Water targets in 2026 USBrC**

| Parameter | Target / range |
| ----- | ----- |
| TDS | 85 mg/L target, 50–125 mg/L accepted |
| Calcium hardness | 51 mg/L target, 17–85 mg/L accepted |
| Alkalinity | 40 mg/L target |
| pH | 7.0 target, 6.5–7.5 accepted |
| Sodium | 10 mg/L target |
| Chlorine / chloramine | 0 mg/L |

Source: 2026 USBrC rules.

**Competitor brings**

Required: brewing devices, filters / filtration media, and Open Service coffee for practice and competition. Optional but relevant: spare devices, scales, thermometers, timers, extra filters, cupping spoons, service vessels for Open Service, cleaning cloths, printed visuals, and up to two added electrical items. The rules also mention optional extra Open Service coffee, with a **2 kg minimum** for attendee service.

**Compulsory coffee quantity**

Each competitor received **350 g** of Compulsory Service coffee right before official practice time, for practice and competition.

**Open Service minimum beverage volume**

Open Service requires three individual hot coffee beverages, one per sensory judge, with at least **120 ml** per judge. Compulsory requires at least **180 ml** per judge.

**Open coffee quantity math**

This is not a rule. It is planning math.

| Use case | Estimate |
| ----- | ----- |
| One Open Service performance, competition brew only | 60–90 g, depending recipe and waste |
| Two Open performances, if Finals reached | 120–180 g |
| Serious dial-in across travel, setup, breakage, roast variation | 800 g–1.5 kg |
| Safe competitor pack without attendee service | 1.5–2.5 kg roasted coffee |
| With attendee service | Add at least 2 kg, per rules |

For a qualifier-style weekend, I would plan **2 kg minimum** for serious practice and service, and **3.5–5 kg** if doing attendee service or multiple roast batches.

### **Coffee transport rules**

For U.S. domestic travel, TSA lists coffee beans / ground coffee as allowed in both carry-on and checked bags. TSA officers still have discretion to separate powders / foods for screening.

For international return into the U.S., USDA APHIS says travelers are permitted to bring unlimited quantities of roasted coffee in luggage without restriction through any U.S. port of entry, but agricultural products must be declared. Green coffee has more restrictions, especially for Hawaii and Puerto Rico.

Practical transport protocol:

* Carry the competition coffee in carry-on, not checked luggage.  
* Use sealed retail bags or labeled vacuum bags.  
* Bring one backup bag in checked luggage only if needed.  
* Avoid ground coffee in large quantities due powder-screening delays.  
* For Bogotá / international Worlds, verify Colombia entry and U.S. re-entry rules before travel.  
* Keep roast date, farm / lot info, and invoice screenshots available.

## **B. Judging / shadowing pathway**

### **WCC Judge Skills Program**

The WCC Judge Skills Program is exactly worth doing. WCC says it is a local-level workshop led by certified WCC Reps, designed for people learning WCC judging and starting toward WCC Certified Judge status. In 2027, it becomes a prerequisite for WCC Judge Certification.

The curriculum covers judging criteria, scoring methods, WCC evaluation systems, current WCC rules / regulations, latest SCA research, and CVA-based evaluation training. WCC also says it is useful for competitors who want a deeper understanding of judging criteria and scoring. Completion gives a WCC \+ SCA digital certificate, but it is **not** WCC Judge Certification.

No official 2027 West Coast session is posted in the sources I found. Monitor WCC’s Judge Skills event page and West Coast schools. Take the **Brewers Cup version** in 2027 if offered.

---

### **WCC Judge Certification**

WCC Certified Judges are required to judge at World Coffee Championships. WCC describes Judge Certification as a **two-day, test-based certification**.

Eligibility is the gate. WCC requires either:

1. Judging experience in one world championship, or  
2. Two years judging the same national-level competition, such as Brewers Cup, Barista, Roasting, Latte Art, or Coffee in Good Spirits.

That experience must be within the last five years. Starting in 2027, a WCC Judge Skills Program certificate is also required unless prior WCC judging experience replaces it.

Implication: for you, WCC Judge Certification is not a 2027 goal. The realistic path is:

2027: JSP \+ volunteer / observe / possibly non-conflicting judge-adjacent work  
 2028: national-level judging path if not competing in that competition year  
 2029+: WCC certification attempt if two eligible national judging years exist

### **Conflict-of-interest rule**

The 2026 USBrC conflict rule is stricter than “same discipline / same season.” It says competitors may not judge in any sanctioned USBrC or WBrC competition, including world, Competition Body, or regional events, in any country before the conclusion of that year’s WBrC. Judges likewise may not compete in any sanctioned WBrC competition before the conclusion of that year’s WBrC.

The same section says competitors who act as calibration baristas are not eligible to compete in a sanctioned event until completion of that competition year. Questions go to **usccleadership@gmail.com**.

Important nuance: “competition year” is tied to the World Championship year the event qualifies into. So the question to ask USCC is not just “2027 season?” It is:

Does this 2027 role attach to the 2027 WBrC competition year or the 2028 WBrC competition year?

Safest 2027 recon: volunteer in a non-judge, non-calibration-barista role unless USCC confirms no conflict with your intended 2028 competitor path.

### **CoE invitation**

Accept it.

Cup of Excellence is not a WBrC brew-service format. It is a rigorous blind cupping competition run through ACE / CoE. ACE says each sample is assigned a number known only to the auditor, each jury member cups blind, and the process includes a National Jury and International Jury over three stages. ACE also states that a 300-entry competition produces roughly 9,000 analyzed cups, with each Top 10 coffee cupped at least 120 times.

It does **not** satisfy WCC Judge Certification eligibility based on the published WCC criteria, because WCC eligibility names world-championship judging or two years judging the same national-level WCC-style competition. CoE is valuable palate density, not WCC eligibility credit.

CVA connection: SCA describes CVA as a four-part assessment system for building a high-resolution picture of coffee value. WCC’s Judge Skills Program explicitly incorporates CVA logic. That makes CoE / CVA-style cupping useful adjacent training even though it is not WBrC service judging.

## **Insights**

1. **The path risk is not cost. It is calendar and slot scarcity.** A $50-ish entry is trivial compared with a sub-1-hour sellout and the need to be ready on launch day.  
2. **The 2027 U.S. calendar matters more than the world calendar.** Bogotá is fixed. The U.S. selection mechanism is the missing piece.  
3. **For Bay Area practicality, Las Vegas wins among known old host cities.** If a 2027 West Coast host appears, reassess. If the map repeats 2025, pick Las Vegas for recon or competition.  
4. **Practice should already account for the LAGOM 01\.** WBrC has a 2026–2029 grinder sponsor cycle. Waiting until the domestic host packet risks months of wasted grinder-specific prep.  
5. **Judging and competing need a firewall.** Volunteer recon is safe. Judging, calibration barista work, coaching, and competitor consultation need written clarification before you commit.  
6. **CoE is the highest ROI palate move.** It gives dense, calibrated exposure to elite coffees. It will not get you WCC certification eligibility, but it improves the sensory foundation behind both competing and judging.
